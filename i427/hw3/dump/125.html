About the course

We hiked the 1000 feet to the top of Sergoit hill on the Kruger Farm just outside of Eldoret on the first weekend with our Kenyan partners.

Students in J418/518 Reporting on HIV/AIDS in Africa were in Eldoret, Kenya, working alongside Moi University students to create multimedia news projects from May 21 to June 12, 2013. Students interviewed rural residents, medical professionals and agency leaders to gather information for the stories presented on this website. For a list of all of their stories, see the feature archives.

Reporting on HIV/AIDS is an advanced reporting course for students with fundamental skills in researching a topic, interviewing human sources, maintaining accurate notes, writing accurate texts, photographing people and places, collecting audio, collecting video of people and places, editing text, photos, audio, and video. It has been offered in 2010, 2011 and 2013.

For the first two weeks, the class meets daily in Ernie Pyle Hall for instruction about the basic pathology of the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), the history of acquired immune deficiency syndrome (AIDS) with particular attention to sub-Saharan Africa, and the current programs and other efforts aimed at addressing the worldwide epidemic. Students also learn about the culture of east Africa and about the media laws of Kenya.

Students then spend three weeks living at the IU House in Eldoret, Kenya. While there they work in two-person teams with communication students from Moi University to report on health care topics in the area with special attention on the various programs of AMPATH, a consortium of Moi University and 10 North American universities lead by Indiana University. After completing their reporting, the IU students travel to Nairobi to meet with editors and reporters who work at the nation’s newspapers and at the international news agencies headquartered in the city. The course finishes in Bloomington where students edit and produce the stories gathered in Kenya.

The course was developed and is taught by Jim Kelly, an associate professor in the IU School of Journalism. His partner in Kenya is Abraham Mulwo, a lecturer in the Department of Communication Studies at Moi University.

In 1989, Indiana University School of Medicine and Moi University School of Medicine joined together to train future generations of health care providers on both continents. At the turn of the century, in the face of the deadliest pandemic in human history, IU and Moi U responded by creating one of Africa’s largest, most comprehensive and effective HIV/AIDS control program called AMPATH: the Academic Model Providing Access to Healthcare. AMPATH treats over 160,000 HIV-positive patients at 23 clinical sites and over 30 satellite sites in both urban and rural Kenya.

AMPATH also:

  • Protects babies by blocking mother-to-child transmission of HIV at birth.
  • Prevents HIV/AIDS through outreach to encourage safe practices, prevent the spread of the disease, and confront the deadly effects of HIV/AIDS stigma.
  • Goes door-to-door throughout western Kenya testing for HIV, screening for tuberculosis and delivering treated bed nets to prevent malaria.
  • Fights hunger using high-production farms to provide food security programs to up to 30,000 people per month.
  • Builds self-sufficiency through income security programs that provide skills training, self-financing, a fair-trade-certified crafts workshop and an agricultural co-operative.
  • Helps orphans and vulnerable children by providing school fees, clothing and food to thousands of Kenyan children orphaned by AIDS.

Learn more at the AMPATH site.

About Eldoret, Kenya

Eldoret is a city of 200,000 in western Kenya in the Rift Valley Province. Lying south of the Cherangani Hills, the local elevation varies from about 7000–9000 feet above sea level. It is the fastest growing city in Kenya and is currently the 5th largest in Kenya.

The name “Eldoret” is based on the Maasai word “eldore” meaning “stony river” because the bed of the nearby Sosiani River is very stony. The white settlers decided to call it Eldoret to make it easier for them to pronounce it. At start of the colonial era, the area was occupied by the Nandi, before that by the Maasai and before that the Sirikwa.

Eldoret was the scene of considerable violence following the November 2007 national elections. In January 2008 a mob attacked and set fire to a church in the city where hundreds of people had taken refuge from mob violence in the streets. As many as 40 people, mostly Kikuyus, were burned to death.

Eldoret is home to Moi University with a student population of 15,000 and the Moi Teaching and Referral Hospital (MTRH), the largest medical facility in the region. Eldoret Polytechnic, the third national polytechnic, is also located in the city.

Eldoret is the hometown of numerous legendary Kenyan runners including Kipchoge Keino. The high altitude is an ideal training ground for many middle and long distance athletes.

About the Alumni Editors

Students were given help editing their stories by two dozen IU Journalism alumni located around the world. Each student submitted one of his or her two draft stories to an editor who had volunteered to help. She or he worked with the student via email to polish and improve the story for publication on the School’s website.
