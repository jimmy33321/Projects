Gender and labor economist Nancy Folbre to present Patten Lectures at IU Bloomington

  • Oct. 26, 2015

FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE

BLOOMINGTON, Ind. -- Economist Nancy Folbre will present two Patten Lectures in November at Indiana University Bloomington. The lectures will explore the intersections of political economy and feminist theory.

Folbre, professor emerita of economics at the University of Massachusetts Amherst, focuses on caring work and other forms of non-market work. Her research has impelled a fundamental re-evaluation of the way economists and social scientists study the intersection of family, labor and the economy.

Her Patten Lectures are free and open to the public. Both will take place from 7:30 to 9 p.m. in Presidents Hall at Franklin Hall. Topics will be:

  • Tuesday, Nov. 10: “Monsters of the Economic: Inequality, Fear and Loathing in America.”
  • Thursday, Nov. 12: “The Political Economy of Patriarchal Systems.”

“Monsters of the Economic” will examine the emotional consequences of extreme income inequality. Folbre will explore how economic insecurity and downward mobility increase susceptibility to political manipulation, spawn fear and loathing, and enforce barriers to solving economic problems like inequality itself. 

In “The Political Economy of Patriarchal Systems,” Folbre will argue that economic theory creates the framework for analyzing the social structures and individual choices that create patriarchies. She will explore how feminist theory can be enhanced with analytical tools such as game theory, bargaining models and exploitation concepts.

Folbre has published numerous books on topics including gender in historical economics, care-sector policies in the U.S., education, the political economy of parenting and the labor sphere within families. She has also published widely in journals such as Science, American Economic Review, Demography, Politics and Society, and Review of Radical Political Economics. From 2009 to 2014, she wrote a weekly blog for The New York Times.

Folbre received a Russell Sage Foundation fellowship and a MacArthur Foundation award, also referred to as the “genius grant.”

The William T. Patten Foundation

The William T. Patten Foundation provides funds to bring distinguished scholars or practitioners in the sciences, the humanities and the arts to the Bloomington campus for a week. The foundation has brought over 150 scholars of extraordinary national and international distinction since 1937, making it the oldest lecture series at Indiana University. Lecturers are chosen by a campus-wide faculty committee.

William T. Patten graduated in 1893 with a B.A. degree in history from IU. He then moved to Indianapolis and led a successful career in real estate and politics. He created an endowment for the university in 1931, with the purpose of bringing renowned leaders to the Bloomington campus.

For more information about Folbre or her lectures, visit the Patten Foundation website or contact vpfaa@indiana.edu.
